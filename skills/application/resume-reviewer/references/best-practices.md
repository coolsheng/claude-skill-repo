# Resume best practices (stable reference)

Principles that hold across roles. For role/industry-specific norms, supplement with a quick web check (Step 3).

## Sections — what to include

Core (almost always): **Contact**, **Experience**, **Education**, **Skills**.
Common add-ons: **Summary** (3–4 lines; valuable for senior/career-changers, skippable for new-grads), **Projects** (strong for students, open-source, or thin work history), **Publications/Patents/Speaking** (research/senior), **Certifications**.
Usually omit: objective statements, references ("available on request"), photo (in US/UK/CA), age/marital status, hobbies unless directly relevant.

Contact essentials: name, email, phone, city (not full address), LinkedIn, and portfolio/GitHub where relevant.

## Section ORDER — depends on seniority

Order communicates what you're selling. Lead with your strongest asset.

- **Student / new-grad:** Contact → (Summary) → Education → Projects → Experience/Internships → Skills. Education leads because it's the strongest signal.
- **Early career (1–5 yrs):** Contact → (Summary) → Experience → Skills → Education → Projects. Experience now outranks education.
- **Mid/Senior (5+ yrs):** Contact → Summary → Experience → Skills → Education (brief, dates often dropped). Education shrinks; impact leads.
- **Career changer:** Lead with a Summary that frames the transition + a Skills/Projects section proving transferable ability, before chronological experience.
- **Academic/research:** Education and Publications rise; the doc may run longer (CV norms).

Within Experience and Education: **reverse-chronological** (most recent first). Within a single entry, order bullets by impact, strongest first — **unless bullets carry their own date ranges (e.g. consulting engagements): keep those reverse-chronological and tailor by expanding/compressing them instead, so the timeline stays readable and parseable**.

## Length

- 1 page for students, new-grads, and <10 yrs experience.
- 2 pages acceptable for senior/extensive experience.
- Academic CVs are an exception (can be long).
- Density matters more than the rule — cut anything that doesn't advance candidacy for the target role.

## Tailoring to the role

- Mirror the language of the target job description (skills, tools, scope) — honestly.
- Move the most role-relevant experience and bullets upward.
- Drop or compress experience irrelevant to the target.
- Match scope to seniority: senior resumes emphasize ownership, leadership, ambiguity, business impact; junior resumes emphasize learning, delivery, and concrete skills.

## Work authorization & relocation

For cross-border applications, work authorization is often the *real* gate — bigger than any bullet. Handle it deliberately:

- If the applicant needs relocation/sponsorship, surface it early and frame the **cheapest, lowest-friction pathway as a selling point**, rather than leaving it an unstated risk recruiters assume the worst about.
- **Australian nationals → US roles: feature the E-3 visa.** It's Australia-only, has **no lottery** (unlike the H-1B), is fast and cheap for employers, and is renewable indefinitely — most US employers don't realize how low-friction it is. A line near the contact info — e.g. *"Eligible for E-3 visa (streamlined US work authorization for Australian nationals; no lottery)"* — converts a likely-disqualifying unknown into a differentiator.
- Mirror the equivalent move for other corridors (e.g. existing right-to-work, TN for Canadians/Mexicans, settled status).
- Only state authorization/eligibility the applicant actually has **and is willing to act on** (e.g. willing to relocate). Never overstate.

## ATS & formatting (machine-readability)

- Single-column, standard section headings ("Experience", not "Where I've Made Magic"). Multi-column and text-in-images often parse badly.
- Standard fonts; no tables/text boxes for core content; avoid headers/footers for essential info.
- Save as PDF unless a system explicitly requests .docx.
- Standardize date format (e.g. "Jan 2023 – Present") and use it everywhere.
- Spell out an acronym once if it's not universal in the field.

## Formatting consistency (visual)

- One date format, one bullet style, consistent verb tense rule (see voice reference), consistent punctuation (bullets either all end in periods or none do).
- Consistent capitalization of job titles, tech, and headings.
- Adequate white space; don't shrink margins/font to cram.

## Common failure modes to flag

- Duty lists instead of achievements ("Responsible for…").
- No quantification anywhere.
- First-person pronouns ("I led…").
- Buzzword soup with no evidence ("synergistic team player").
- Inconsistent tense or date formats.
- Wall-of-text bullets (>2 lines).
- Same generic resume sent to every role (no tailoring).
- Claims that under- or over-shoot the target seniority.
